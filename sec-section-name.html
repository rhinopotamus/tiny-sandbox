    Exercises PROTEUS exercises (for Section 5.2)

    1.

    Consider the function defined by the rule
    \begin{equation*} A(x) = \int_{0.5}^x f(t)\, dt\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In the figure below, \(f(t)\) is the function graphed in blue, and \(A(3)\) is found by computing the area shaded in gray.
    🔗
    (a)
    For some small \(\Delta x\text{,}\) shade in the area corresponding to \(A(3+\Delta x)\) on the figure above.
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    As long as \(\Delta x\) is relatively small, \(\Delta A = A(3+\Delta x) - A(3)\) is well approximated by a rectangle. Draw such a rectangle on the figure above, and label its height and its width.
    🔗
    🔗
    (c)
    Since \(\Delta A\) is approximately a rectangle, \(\Delta A\) is approximately the product of the height and the width. Use your labels from the previous part to write an approximation for \(\Delta A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    (d)
    What is \(\dfrac{\Delta A}{\Delta x}\text{?}\)

    Exercises PROTEUS exercises (for Section 5.3)

    Each of the following expressions is the result of the Chain Rule being applied to a composite function. In other words, it is the right-hand side of the Chain Rule:
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{d}{dx} \left[ F(g(x)) \right] = f(g(x)) g'(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Drag the blocks in each problem below to identify the functions \(f\text{,}\) \(g\text{,}\) and \(g'\text{,}\) and then calculate the original composite function.
    🔗

    1.

    \begin{equation*} 2 (2x+5)^7 = f(g(x)) g'(x) \end{equation*}
    Drag \(f(x)\text{,}\) \(g(x)\text{,}\) \(g'(x)\text{,}\) and \(F(g(x))\) into the box below, in that order.
    🔗
    🔗

    2.

    \begin{equation*} \sin\left(e^x\right) e^x = f (g(x) ) g'(x) \end{equation*}
    Drag \(f(x)\text{,}\) \(g(x)\text{,}\) \(g'(x)\text{,}\) and \(F(g(x))\) into the box below, in that order.
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    \begin{equation*} 3x^2 e^{x^3+2} = f (g(x) ) g’(x) \end{equation*}
    Drag \(f(x)\text{,}\) \(g(x)\text{,}\) \(g'(x)\text{,}\) and \(F(g(x))\) into the box below, in that order.
